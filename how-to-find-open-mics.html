HOW TO FIND LOCAL OPEN MICS

April 3rd, 2021 | Keyla Jones

By Keyla Jones


Introduction

Let me start by saying that I am so proud of you for wanting to get out of your comfort zone and improve your musical and life skills! It is not easy to get up in front of a crowd and sing, but this how-to guide is going to provide you with everything you need to help you be a success. From finding the right type of open mic for your genre and skill level to giving you all the pro tips you might not have thought of. After all, no one wants to be a little teen popstar singing a love song in a biker bar.


Sections:

  • Different Types of Open Mics
  • How to Find Local Open Mics

The Different Types of Open Mics

Not all open mics are made equally. Some can be rough, inspiring, fun, and just plain awkward. This boils down to the venue, audience, other performers, and different musical levels. This quick guide will help you pick the best venue for you.



1. Local Community Open Mic Night

Skill Level: Newbie and Medium
Atmosphere: Outgoing, upbeat.
Typical Restrictions: None
Age Requirement: Open to all ages

This kind of venue reminds me of community theaters (not just because they are commonly held in community theaters!). They are open to all ages, all levels, and all types of performance. They might have poetry, rap, slam poetry, or really anything. This makes them a great place for starting out because everyone is super open, supportive, and will go out of their way to help you grow. You will feel like part of the family and make friends for life.



2. Coffee Shop/Small Cafe

Skill Level: Newbie and Medium
Atmosphere: Laid back, chill.
Typical Restrictions: Must play an instrument or sing acapella, genre restrictions, may restrict to acoustic only.
Age Requirement: Open to all ages

These venues have the widest range of skill levels, you may go from the cringiest player to the next Taylor Swift. Typically, open to all ages, but restricted to musical performers and certain genres (Such as they don’t allow heavy metal or rap). Since there is such a mix of skill levels, it’s a great place to make contacts and find mentors to help you grow.



3. Bars/Biker Bars

Skill Level: High/Expert
Atmosphere: Intimidating, friendly, non-musical*
Typical Restrictions: Age restrictions, genre restrictions, no acoustic or acapella.
Age Requirement: 21+ (Since they are bars)
*Non-musical is when the audience isn’t full of other musicians.

The reason this skill level is so high is due to the fact of the non-musical audience. Musicians are very supportive of other musicians (because they have been through the same thing as you). Whereas, none-musicians can be very judgemental and don’t understand when you mess up.
This kind of place, while fun, can be very intimidating due to the rough crowd. It’s not really a place for the next Taylor Swift. These venues are great for upbeat songs the audience can jam to while they drink. Not a place for slow, sad, heartbreak melodies. While it was intimidating in my experience, after I performed, I had many of the audience members come over to talk to me and ask for links to my music.



4. Pubs/Family Restaurants

Skill Level: High/Expert
Atmosphere: Intimidating, non-musical*
Typical Restrictions: Age restrictions, genre restrictions, no acoustic or acapella.
Age Requirement: 16+ (They don’t typically allow kids to perform)
*Non-musical is when the audience isn’t full of other musicians.

Maybe your next to dive into the deep in but a bar isn’t really your scene? This type of open mic is great for seasoned musicians that know how to capture a crowd’s attention. Like the bars, most of the audience isn’t musicians themselves, so they can be a tough crowd.
This is the kind of place the average family goes to for a night out, to watch live entertainment, or a nice place for tourists to stop in.
Most are there to eat, not really to hear you play, but it’s a great bonus. With this in mind, if they don’t like what you are playing, they will tune you out, so you have to be okay with playing for no one.

How to Find Open Mics

Finding the best open mic nights involve a bit of research, here are my best tricks to help you.



1. If you have friends that are also musicians, ask them for the best spots.

2. Try to find some local musicians on social media and send them a message to ask. Typically musicians are super friendly and happy to give advice.

3. Google is your new best friend. Try searching for “open mic in -your city here-”. If google doesn’t automatically give you a list of local places, then you’ll probably find a few sites with pages full of them.

Once you’ve found a few options, Check their website and social media for any upcoming dates and times. Then call to confirm it’s still going on, sometimes they get cancelled last minute.

Things to keep an eye out for:

1. Age requirements

2. Cover fee This means they charge you to either attend or perform. Sometimes, it will only cost you money if you don’t perform but it’s always best to call and ask before going.

3. Genre only Some venues have limits to what can be performed such as no rap, heavy metal, or poetry.

4. Acoustic only Some venues are small and don’t have sound equipment but still love live music. Thus, acoustic only venues were born. This means it will just be you and your acoustic instrument.

5. Song limit or time restraint All venues either have a song limit or time restraint such as only 1-2 songs or 8 minutes. These allow everyone a chance to perform.

6. For higher level venues Look up other performers to get an idea of what the venue is looking for..

7. When sign up starts Typically, 15-30 minutes before the start time.



Tips and Tricks for Performing Live

By this point, you should have found a great local open mic that fits your genre and skill level! Now let’s talk about the expert tips and tricks to make a great first impression and rock your performance.

1. Get there early
Depending where you live and how many people frequent the open mic will help you decide how early you need to be there to sign up for a time slot. In Seattle, it could be an hour, but in a small town in the south, 10-15 minutes is fine.

2. Should you start or finish the show?
Before you decide to be the grand finale, keep in mind song limits, If there are 8 people in front of you on the list and they get 2 songs each, that’s almost an hour before you go on and you might lose your nerve.

3. Do I stay or do I go?
It’s always polite to try to stay to the end if you can, that way everyone has an audience to perform for. But we know that is not always possible, so make sure you wait until there is a break between performers before you sneak out the back.

4. Practice makes perfect!
Practice your song as much as you can before the show, maybe even put a video on and try to sing over the background noise. If you mess up, take a beat and keep going! Most of the time people don’t notice or care about the mistake as much as you think they do.

Last Notes

We’ve all started from the bottom, we all remember how intimidating it was playing for a crowd the first time. That’s why musicians are so supportive of each other, we’ve all been there. Some nights will go better than others, but just keep going, it’s a skill you have to work on and build. I like to keep a recording of each performance to see what it looked like from the audience's view, plus you can look back and see how much you’ve grown. You got this!



Best Wishes,

Keyla Jones
